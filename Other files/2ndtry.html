Spectrum of thinking interventions

How our mind would respond in a default, lowest energy way, if we don't consciously do something different. From situations requiring minimal thought to those demanding intensive thinking

  1. Habits: a learned routine based on familiar cues
  2. Other intuitive responses: responses based on past experiences in a familiar, semi-familiar situations
  3. Active mindset or self-concept: used in ambiguous scenarios with multiple possible interventions
  4. Heuristics: used in situations requiring conscious attention, but the decisions can be made easily
  5. Focus, conscious calculation: used in unfamiliar scenarios or crucial decisions where deliberate focus is required
    6.

NUDGE Theory

a behavioral science concept that focuses on understanding how humans make choices and how they can be influenced by subtle changes in the environment (than direct instruction, legislation or enforcement).

  1. Biases in decision-making processes

    • Anchoring bias: people make decisions based heavily on the first piece of information given to them
    • Availability bias: people think that things are more likely to happen, if we can easily remember experiences of when they happened before
    • Representativeness heuristics: We assume things that fit our stereotype to be more common [or more rational]

  2. NUDGE methods

    • Choice Architecture: Design layouts and ordering of choices that influences user decisions, like placing desirable features on top
    • Default: Design default options/modes that encourage users to make beneficial choices
    • Social proof: showing people making choices before and/or having positive experiences
    • Timely interventions: providing information at critical moments to guide user decisions
    • Narrowing the field: reducing the number of choices presented to users to simplify decision-making, like making lists, categories, and other methods
    • Feedback: providing users with information about their actions to reinforce learning and improve decision-making

Persuasive design

Persuasive design is an UX area focuses on influencing human behaviors and encouraging users to take specific actions using technology.

  1. Examples of persuasive design techniques

    • Reinforcement: positive feedbacks/rewards towards completing an action [a badge, score, points,...]
    • Social influence: leveraging social connections/norms to encourage actions, like create a friend leaderboards, nudge friend options
    • Scarcity: present the concept of scarcity, like limited offers, to instill a sense of urgency and FOMO
    • Personalization: tailoring experiences and content to individual user preferences and behaviors
    • Commitment and consistency: encouraging users to make small commitments that lead to larger actions over time
